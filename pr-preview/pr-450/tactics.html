Tactics That Matter

Flanking, morale and routing, the unit triangle, terrain, and disengaging.
Published

Last updated: 2026-07-01

Numbers aren’t everything. Sparta’s battles are decided by where units are and which way they face — flanking, morale, and the rock-paper-scissors of unit types turn an even fight lopsided.

Flanking

Hitting a unit from the side or rear deals far more damage and morale loss than a frontal clash:

Attack angle Multiplier
Front ×1.0
Side (flank) ×1.5
Rear ×2.0

Maneuver behind the enemy line. A regiment pinned frontally by a screen while your cavalry hits its rear will melt — and start a rout that spreads.

Morale & routing

Units don’t fight to the last soldier. When a unit loses enough soldiers or takes flank/rear hits, it routs — it flees (shown faded) and stops counting toward the battle.

  • Routs are contagious. A nearby ally watching a unit break is more likely to break too. One collapse can cascade down a line.
  • This makes morale a weapon: you can win by breaking the enemy’s will before you’ve killed even half their soldiers.

Rank cycling (trained units)

A well-trained melee unit periodically rotates its ranks — the tired front line steps back and a fresh rank takes its place, signalled by a whistle. Cycling slows fatigue buildup (by up to half at full training) and lets the unit recover morale even while still fighting. The better trained the unit, the more often it cycles. Ranged units don’t cycle.

Fatigue builds at a realistic pace — sustained melee wears a unit down over minutes, not seconds, and resting in the rear restores it just as gradually. So relief is a deliberate decision for a prolonged fight, not a frantic every-few-seconds chore.

Training therefore pays off in a long grind: a drilled regiment wears down a green one of equal numbers, because the green unit tires and wavers first.

Stamina

Every soldier carries a stamina pool that drains through combat and refills at rest. Each blow thrown costs stamina; throwing blows costs more than absorbing them. Rising from the ground after being knocked down costs a burst extra. Low stamina lowers the soldier’s condition factor, which scales both attack output and defence — a tired soldier hits softer and is easier to hit.

So a unit that has been grinding at the front for a while is measurably weaker than one that just arrived fresh. Rank cycling (above) helps by rotating tired soldiers to the rear to recover; simply pulling a unit out of melee and letting it rest works too.

Prone / knockdown

A blow that delivers enough force can knock a soldier off their feet. A prone soldier has no active defence — only their armour absorbs incoming hits — and must spend both time (about two seconds) and stamina to get back up.

Visually, prone soldiers stand out:

  • At normal zoom (mark LOD): a prone soldier’s mark is flattened into a wide horizontal sliver so it reads clearly as “lying flat” among the standing marks.
  • Zoomed in (figure LOD): the figure silhouette is rotated 90° to show a person lying on their side.

Engaged lines resist knockdown. A unit that is actively locked in melee (and not in Skirmish stance) braces automatically, raising the impulse threshold needed to knock a soldier flat. A skirmishing or unengaged unit does not brace and is easier to topple. A deeper file also helps — more soldiers absorbing the same front-rank pressure means fewer individual soldiers pushed past the knockdown threshold.

A cluster of prone soldiers in the front rank is a warning sign: your line is tired or outmatched there, and each fallen body briefly opens a gap in your defence before they can rise.

Line width (frontage)

You set how wide each regiment fights — [ and ] step the width by a file, or drag a selected unit’s flank grips. The same soldiers spread across a different shape:

  • Wide and shallow puts more men in the front rank, so more of them are swinging at once — good for overlapping an enemy’s flank or covering ground so a foe can’t slip around your line.
  • Narrow and deep stacks more ranks behind a thinner front. Fewer fight at any moment, but the column has more bodies to feed forward as the front falls — it holds a chokepoint or punches through one spot rather than spreading thin.

A wider line is easier to outlast in depth and quicker to crumble once it bends; a deeper one trades frontage for staying power. The width holds as casualties thin the ranks, so a line you set wide stays wide until you change it.

Deploy a whole battle line at once. Select several regiments and drag one flank line (right-click and drag): the line splits among them. By default it splits to equal depth, so every regiment forms up the same number of ranks — a uniform front where bigger units just take a wider slice. That keeps your whole line’s staying power even, with no thin spot for the enemy to punch through. Press Y to cycle to equal width when you’d rather every unit cover the same frontage (small units stack deeper). They sort left-to-right by where they already stand, so nobody crosses the field to reach a slot — a fast way to dress a ragged group into one clean front before the enemy closes. Hold Shift to place them in selection order instead when you want a specific regiment on a specific flank.

Use ☰ Menu → Form-up to configure the split: the upper radio buttons set the mode each battle starts in; the lower checkboxes (“Y-key cycles through…”) control which modes Y steps between mid-battle. Uncheck a mode to skip it entirely from the cycle.

The unit triangle (rock-paper-scissors)

Three roles, each strong against one and weak to another:

🗡️ Infantry

The reliable line. No special bonus — your anvil to pin the enemy in place while something else does the killing.

🐎 Cavalry

Fast — they charge at a real gallop (~8.5 m/s, far quicker than any foot unit’s advance) — with a charge bonus that grows with closing speed. Devastating into a flank or rear, but blunted when it charges into braced spears.

🛡️ Spearmen

Anti-cavalry (marked with a spear). They blunt the cavalry charge, so use them to screen your line against enemy horse. Their long spears also out-reach shorter weapons, landing the first blows as lines close (see below).

The loop: cavalry beats exposed flanks → spearmen beat cavalry → infantry pin so your cavalry can get around to those flanks. Read the enemy’s screen before you commit your horse.

Weapon reach

Each weapon has an effective reach — how close two units must get before they trade blows. A longer weapon reaches that contact distance first, so it lands the opening strikes of a clash while a shorter-weapon enemy is still closing the gap:

  • Spears reach furthest (~2.4 m) — spearmen strike first as the lines meet, on top of blunting cavalry charges.
  • Swords (infantry, cavalry) are the mid-length baseline (~1.3–1.5 m).
  • Archers carry only a short sidearm (~0.6 m); they’re meant to shoot, not duel in the press.

So a spear line meeting a sword line head-on gets free hits during the approach — another reason to lead with spearmen and let the shorter weapons pin and flank. Combat now resolves per soldier by weapon reach: a spearman strikes any enemy within its longer reach, while a swordsman beyond its own (shorter) reach cannot strike back. The sustained standoff — a spear line physically holding swordsmen out at weapon length — now emerges from knockback: a landed blow shoves the struck soldier back, so a spear line keeps knocking swordsmen out past their own reach while staying in its. The lines no longer snap apart by a spacing rule; the gap is the live balance of the press forward against the knockback, so it breathes as the fight ebbs and flows.

Movement speed

Units move at realistic real-world speeds, measured in metres per second, with three independent paces per type — walk, jog, and sprint (see Pace modes below) — scaled to how much each type’s panoply weighs:

Type Walk Jog Sprint
Spearmen (heaviest kit) 1.1 m/s 1.8 m/s 2.8 m/s
Infantry 1.3 m/s 2.5 m/s 4.0 m/s
Archers (lightest kit) 1.5 m/s 3.0 m/s 4.5 m/s
Cavalry 1.7 m/s (walk gait) 3.5 m/s (trot) 8.5 m/s (gallop)

Heavier armor and weapons cost proportionally more at a run than at a walk — walking pace barely changes under load, but running form degrades fast, so spearmen’s walk sits close to the lighter types’ while their sprint lags well behind. Cavalry’s three paces map onto the horse’s own real gaits (walk / trot / gallop) rather than a rider-weight-scaled fraction, since a rider’s kit is a small fraction of what the horse itself carries.

That sprint gap is a lever: cavalry cross the field and reach an exposed flank far sooner than infantry can re-form to face them, so commit horse decisively and time your foot to be where the charge lands. (The faster the gallop at the moment of contact, the bigger the charge bonus — so a clean run-up hits harder than a standing start.)

Pace modes

By default, foot units advance on AUTO pace: they walk on the approach, jog when a ranged enemy is firing at them (to spend less time under missiles), and sprint at full speed once within about 200 px of the target — so a charge arrives at full momentum without wearing out the troops on the march.

Toggle Walk advance (☰ Menu → Walk advance) to lock units to walk pace throughout. This is required for formed stances like shield wall or pike phalanx: those formations hold their shape only at a walk, and an auto-sprint will break them. Keep Walk advance on whenever you are using a tight formation that would crumble if the unit broke into a run.

The AUTO sprint-override also matters for charge timing: a unit that enters sprint range will accelerate through any missile fire it still faces — gaining maximum speed at contact rather than jogging through the kill zone. Use that to your advantage when committing cavalry or an assault infantry charge.

Side-stepping the line

A small lateral move order is carried out as a side-step: the unit shuffles sideways while holding its facing, instead of pivoting to face the move and pivoting back. That matters when you are dressing a line just before contact. Pivoting a regiment turns it away from the enemy for the length of the turn — its front momentarily points sideways, and the ranks lose their dressing while the soldiers reshuffle. A side-step keeps every soldier looking at the enemy and keeps the line aligned, so you can nudge a unit a few paces to close a gap or overlap an enemy flank without ever presenting your own flank in the process.

The cut-off is roughly a unit’s own width: shifts wider than that, or moves that head forward, turn and march normally. So to reposition laterally in good order, make the adjustment small — a short step keeps the line intact, a long one commits the unit to a march.

The centre pivot

A larger turn is a centre pivot: the unit rotates about its own centre onto its new heading in good order rather than snapping round. When the turn is sharp the unit pivots before it advances — it nearly halts, faces its destination, then marches — so it never slides sideways or backwards at speed. A destination to the rear therefore triggers a full 180° pivot: the whole regiment turns around to face where it is going before it steps off. (Don’t confuse that with the conversio about-face on V, where each soldier turns in place and the block does not rotate.)

The cost is time: a sharp reorientation spends a beat pivoting before the unit makes ground, and a 180° pivot spends the most. Plan turns ahead of contact — a unit told to reverse at the moment of impact will spend its pivot facing the wrong way. Note that a chasing or charging unit turns instantly instead, so an attack order never pays the pivot tax; the orderly centre pivot is reserved for your own move orders, where keeping the ranks dressed is worth the beat.

The pivot itself also slows as the unit picks up speed — a unit turns fastest from a stand and tightens its turn rate the faster it’s already moving (down to 40% of the stationary rate at a full sprint), reflecting the real lateral force a moving body or formation can exert without losing footing or cohesion. So a turn ordered from a standstill snaps around quickest; one ordered mid-charge takes noticeably longer to come about.

Speed itself isn’t instant either: a unit ramps up to its target pace (walk, jog, or sprint) and ramps down when slowing or stopping, rather than snapping to a new speed. Heavier-kitted troops (spearmen) build up speed slower than lighter ones (archers), and every foot type slows down faster than it speeds up — stopping takes no effort, building momentum does. Cavalry is closer to symmetric: a galloping horse can’t be reined in much faster than it can build up speed in the first place.

Order stances

Each order can carry a stance, armed with a hotkey before you click (a coloured cursor shows which is active; Esc clears it). Stances turn the same units into different tools:

  • Hold (H) — the unit stands its ground instead of chasing bait. Anchor a line, or keep a screen in place while your cavalry swings wide.
  • Attack flank (F) / Attack rear (R) — the unit maneuvers for the side or back rather than the front, cashing in the ×1.5 / ×2 multipliers above. Pair it with a frontal pin.
  • Skirmish (K) — a ranged unit kites: it backs off when a threat closes inside its firing range, trading ground to keep shooting rather than being caught in melee.
  • Support (G) — the unit guards a chosen ally, shadowing it and intercepting any enemy that closes on it. Good for gluing spearmen to vulnerable horse, or a guard onto your archers.

A volley is not instant: the arrows fly a real arc and land where they were aimed a moment later, so a fast unit can partly outrun a shot loosed at long range, and casualties arrive on the beat the arrows come down rather than the instant they leave the bow.

Group attack distribution

When you have several units selected and right-click an enemy, press X to cycle between two attack modes:

  • Focused (default) — every selected unit charges the same target. Use it to overwhelm a single regiment quickly.
  • Distributed — each unit picks a different nearby enemy, sorted by proximity to the clicked target. Extra units cycle through the list if you have more units than enemies. Use it to hold an entire line rather than piling onto one regiment and leaving the rest unengaged.

The flash message in the top-centre of the screen confirms the active mode. The mode is sticky — it stays set until you press X again.

Terrain

Two types of terrain patch appear on the battle field:

Patch Colour Effect
Forest Dark green Passable - units can enter, but movement is slowed to 60% of normal speed
Hill Tan Impassable - units route around it via A* pathfinding

Units automatically path around impassable terrain when ordered to move. The forest is a different trade-off: routing around it costs distance; routing through it costs time. Generally it is faster to push cavalry or fast-moving infantry through rather than around a narrow forest.

TipForests slow cavalry charges

A cavalry unit that contacts the enemy while still inside a forest hits with a reduced charge bonus — it can’t build full speed in the trees. Stage your charges from open ground whenever possible.

Order-response delay

Units don’t react instantly to orders. There is a brief reaction delay before they execute — about 0.5 s for infantry and spearmen, 0.3 s for cavalry. A unit already in melee is not slowed by the delay.

This models the time for orders to travel down the chain of command. In practice:

  • Commit cavalry early. If you wait until the last moment to redirect horse, they may not reach the flank in time.
  • Feints are risky. A unit you pull away from melee takes rear hits during the disengage and takes a moment to start moving — don’t disengage into a second threat without enough margin.

Disengaging — a risky lever

Right-click the ground (a move order) to pull a unit out of melee. It’s how you retreat a battered regiment or redeploy cavalry to a fresh flank.

WarningDisengaging exposes your back

While a unit marches away it shows its rear to whatever it left — so that enemy gets free rear hits (×2) until your unit is clear. Pull out at the right moment, or pull out into a disaster.

Stop the unit and it re-engages anything nearby. Timing the disengage — late enough that the enemy is committed, early enough that you survive the exit — is one of the game’s sharper decisions.
